  Acute Radiation Syndrome: Information for Clinicians

Source: CDC Radiation Emergencies. URL: https://www.cdc.gov/radiation-emergencies/hcp/clinical-guidance/ars.html
Last reviewed: April 23, 2024

   Overview

Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS) (sometimes known as acute radiation sickness) is an acute illness caused by radiation exposure of the entire body (or most of the body) by a high dose of penetrating radiation in a very short period of time (usually a matter of minutes). The major cause is depletion of immature parenchymal stem cells in specific tissues.

   Conditions for ARS

- The radiation dose must be large (greater than 0.7 Gray (Gy) or 70 rads). Mild symptoms may be observed as low as 0.3 Gy or 30 rads.
- The dose usually must be external and penetrating (high energy X-rays, gamma rays, neutrons).
- The entire body (or a significant portion) must have received the dose.
- The dose must have been delivered in a short time (usually minutes).

   The three classic acute radiation syndromes

- Bone marrow (hematopoietic) syndrome: full syndrome usually 0.7–10 Gy (70–1000 rads). Primary cause of death is destruction of bone marrow, resulting in infection and hemorrhage.
- Gastrointestinal (GI) syndrome: full syndrome usually  10 Gy (1000 rads). Survival extremely unlikely; death usually within 2 weeks.
- Cardiovascular (CV)/Central Nervous System (CNS) syndrome: full syndrome usually  50 Gy (5000 rads). Death may occur within 3 days.

   The four stages of ARS

- Prodromal stage (N-V-D): nausea, vomiting, anorexia, possibly diarrhea, minutes to days after exposure.
- Latent stage: patient looks and feels healthy for hours up to weeks.
- Manifest illness stage: symptoms depend on the specific syndrome; last hours to months.
- Recovery or death.

   Patient management — Triage

If radiation exposure is suspected:
- Secure ABCs (airway, breathing, circulation) and physiologic monitoring.
- Treat major traumatic burns and respiratory injuries if evident.
- Obtain blood samples for CBC with attention to lymphocyte count, and HLA typing prior to any transfusion.
- Provide decontamination for external contamination if necessary.
- If exposure occurred within 8 to 12 hours, repeat CBC 2 or 3 more times (every 2 to 3 hours) to assess lymphocyte depletion.
